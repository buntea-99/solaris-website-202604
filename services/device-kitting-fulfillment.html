What device kitting actually means

Device kitting is the process of receiving bulk devices from the OEM, configuring each unit (OS image, MDM enrolment, SIM activation, accessory pairing), applying asset tags, packaging into per-employee or per-site kits, and shipping direct to the end recipient. Fulfillment covers the chain-of-custody logistics, tracking, and proof-of-delivery for the entire batch. Without a kitting partner, every device requires manual IT staging time after delivery; on a 1,000-device fleet that translates to hundreds of hours of IT labour.

Solaris's kitting workflow is designed around the institutional buyer who wants the device to arrive at the employee's desk fully provisioned, MDM-enrolled, asset-tagged, and ready to use within 60 seconds of unboxing.

Scope of a typical Solaris kitting engagement

  • OEM allocation: direct-from-OEM allocation of the agreed device mix (iPhone, Pixel, Samsung Galaxy, Sonim, Kyocera, Motorola, ruggedised handsets, laptops, tablets, IoT, accessories).
  • Custom OS imaging: Android open-source flashing, vendor-locked OS, carrier-specific firmware, default APN configuration, splash-screen branding.
  • MDM auto-enrolment registration: every IMEI/serial pre-registered in your Apple Business Manager, Google Zero-Touch or Samsung Knox tenant.
  • SIM activation and pairing: for MVNO and operator engagements, each device pre-paired to the subscriber's SIM and account.
  • Asset tagging: barcode, QR, RFID applied to your numbering scheme; tag-to-IMEI mapping delivered as a CSV.
  • Accessory pairing: chargers, cables, cases, screen protectors, peripherals (Bluetooth scanners, payment devices) packed per-recipient.
  • Tamper-evident packaging: per-recipient kit packaged with serialised seal and chain-of-custody label.
  • Direct-to-employee shipping: per-recipient address fulfilment with tracking, signature confirmation and proof-of-delivery callback to your operations team.
  • Reverse logistics: off-boarding retrieval, secure data wipe to NIST 800-88, certified e-waste disposal or refurbish-and-redeploy.

Engagements where Solaris kitting is most used

  • Distributed-workforce enterprise rollouts: Fortune 500 hybrid and remote-first organisations shipping iPhones, Pixels and Samsung Galaxy direct to employee addresses across the United States.
  • MVNO subscriber-acquisition fulfilment: Republic Wireless, Pacific MVNO operators (including Cook Islands operators) shipping pre-activated handsets direct to new subscribers.
  • Restaurant kiosk programmes: the Ritual.co kiosk programme, close to 10,000 mobile-phone kiosk units across three continents, kitted with custom POS firmware, mounting accessories and per-restaurant configuration.
  • Government and federal agency rollouts: TAA-compliant devices kitted, asset-tagged to the agency scheme and shipped via approved carriers with full chain-of-custody documentation.
  • Healthcare and field-service fleets: ruggedised handsets and tablets pre-configured for the field-service application stack, asset-tagged for the hospital or service-fleet inventory system.

Why Solaris kitting differs from a generic third-party logistics provider

Generic 3PL providers handle the warehousing and shipping but rarely touch the device. Solaris is an OEM-direct distributor; the same team that procures the device also images it, registers it for auto-enrolment, asset-tags it and ships it. There is no handoff between procurement and fulfilment, which removes the misalignment that causes most failed enterprise rollouts (devices arrive with wrong OS image, missing MDM registration, mis-tagged inventory).

For institutional buyers who already use a 3PL for warehousing, Solaris ships the kitted devices to the 3PL warehouse on the buyer's instruction. For buyers without an in-house logistics partner, Solaris's Miami facility handles end-to-end fulfilment from procurement through direct-to-employee delivery.

Lead time and engagement model

Specialist hardware (Sonim, Kyocera, Zebra, Honeywell, body cameras) lead time is confirmed at proposal stage.
