## Video 1

### Working title
**What Is a Google-Approved Electronic Device Vendor (and Why It Matters for Buyers)**

### Target keyword
google approved electronic device vendor

### Target audience
Procurement officers, IT directors, supply chain managers evaluating bulk device suppliers. People who have been burned by a wholesaler before and want to understand what vendor approval programmes actually verify.

### Recording notes
- 8 to 10 minutes spoken at a calm pace, roughly 1,200 to 1,500 words.
- Founder on camera, plain background, natural light. iPhone in landscape on a small tripod, eye level.
- Pause for half a second between sections. Do not rush the 2016 origin story; that is the hook.
- No slides required. If you want b-roll, hold up a feature phone or a sealed device box at the timestamps marked **[B-ROLL]**.

### Thumbnail concept
Founder on the left, looking at camera, holding a single mobile phone. On the right, large clean text: **"GOOGLE-APPROVED VENDOR"** with a small subline **"What it actually means."** No stock-photo gradients, no arrows.

---

### Full spoken script

**0:00**
If you are a procurement officer or an IT director, and you are evaluating an electronic device supplier, somewhere on their website you have probably seen the phrase "Google-approved vendor". I want to spend the next ten minutes explaining what that actually means, what it does not mean, and why it should matter to you as a buyer.

**0:30**
My name is [INSERT NAME]. I am the founder of Solaris Wireless. We are an electronic device supplier and distributor based in Miami. We have been operating since 2013, and we became a Google-approved vendor in 2016. So this is not a marketing claim for us. It is the actual programme our team has been working inside for almost a decade.

**1:00**
Let me start with the origin story, because it explains what the approval is for.

**1:15**
In 2016, Google came to us with a problem. They needed a specific feature phone, the kind of low-cost handset most people do not think about anymore, and the model they wanted had been discontinued by the manufacturer. Discontinued in the consumer channel. Out of distribution. Not on Amazon, not on a carrier shelf, not in a wholesaler's standard catalogue.

**1:45**
Google did not need ten of them. They needed a meaningful institutional volume, sourced legitimately, with a paper trail clean enough to satisfy their procurement and compliance teams. That is a very different sourcing problem from what you do on a consumer marketplace. [B-ROLL: hold up a feature phone]

**2:15**
We sourced the units. We documented provenance. We delivered. And from that single project, we were invited into Google's approved vendor programme for electronic devices. We have been on it since.

**2:40**
So what does Google's vendor approval actually measure. There are roughly four things, and I want to walk through each one because most buyers I talk to do not realise how specific the criteria are.

**3:00**
First, financial stability. Google does not want to award a multi-million dollar device order to a company that might not exist in six months. They look at trading history, banking references, audited accounts where applicable. They want to see that you have been doing this for years, not months.

**3:30**
Second, sourcing legitimacy. This is the big one. Where do your devices come from. Are you buying from authorised channels. Can you produce import paperwork, OEM correspondence, distributor agreements. The grey-market device economy is enormous and Google has zero tolerance for it. They need to know that every unit you supply could be traced back to a clean origin.

**4:10**
Third, operational capacity. Can you actually fulfil at the volume and on the timeline they need. This is where a lot of small wholesalers fall out. Saying you can ship 5,000 units across three regions in fourteen days is one thing. Demonstrating that you have the warehousing, the logistics partners, and the staff to do it repeatedly is another.

**4:45**
Fourth, compliance and data handling. If you are touching enrolled devices, MDM provisioning, ABM or Knox enrolment, you are touching customer data. Even just serial numbers and IMEIs. Google wants to see that you have process discipline around that.

**5:15**
Now here is what Google's approval does not mean, and I think this is just as important.

**5:30**
It does not mean you are the cheapest. Vendor approval has nothing to do with price. You can be a Google-approved vendor and be more expensive than a grey-market reseller on Alibaba. The whole point of the programme is that price is not the only variable.

**6:00**
It also does not mean Google endorses you for everything. Approval is scoped. We are approved as an electronic device supplier. That is a specific lane. It does not make us a Google partner in the marketing sense, and we are careful not to overstate it.

**6:30**
And it does not mean Google checks every shipment. The approval is a verification of your business as an institution, not a continuous audit of every transaction. Once you are in, the responsibility is on you to keep operating to the standard that got you in.

**7:00**
So why should this matter to you as a buyer.

**7:15**
If you are sourcing devices in volume, and especially if you are sourcing for an institution where compliance, asset tracking, and warranty integrity matter, the Google-approved status is one of the few external signals you can use to filter suppliers. It is not the only signal, but it is one of the harder ones to fake. The criteria I just walked through are real, and they take years to satisfy.

**7:50**
Practically, what I would tell a buyer is this. When you are evaluating a supplier, ask them three questions. One, how long have you been in business. Two, can you show me the procurement chain on a sample SKU, all the way back to the OEM or authorised distributor. Three, what is your fulfilment capacity in units per month, broken down by region. Suppliers who can answer those three cleanly are the ones worth talking to.

**8:30**
At Solaris, we have shipped over 100,000 mobile units into more than twenty countries. We supply Fortune 500 companies, MVNOs, government, and specialist hardware programmes. We operate out of five supply nodes: Miami, the Netherlands, Dubai, Hong Kong, and Singapore. The Google approval is one piece of why institutional buyers work with us, but the operational track record matters at least as much.

**9:00**
If you want the deeper write-up on what Google's vendor programme involves and how to evaluate suppliers against it, we have a long-form article on our site. The link is in the description: solariswireless.com slash blog slash google-approved-vendor-electronic-devices.

**9:30**
And if you have a sourcing problem of your own, a discontinued model, a custom OS requirement, a multi-region rollout, the contact form on our homepage is the fastest way to reach us. We respond within one business day.

**9:50**
Thanks for watching.

## Video 2

### Working title
**How MVNOs Source Wholesale Android Phones (the 5-step institutional workflow)**

### Target keyword
mvno wholesale android sourcing

### Target audience
MVNO operations directors, head of devices at virtual operators, procurement leads at carriers and resellers. People who already know what an MVNO is and want a substantive walkthrough of how the device side actually runs.

### Recording notes
- 10 to 12 minutes, roughly 1,500 to 1,800 words.
- This is the most technical of the three. Speak slowly on the OEM names so the captions get them right.
- If possible, hold up a sample device at the SIM-pairing section [B-ROLL marker].
- Avoid naming subscriber counts or specific commercial terms for any client. Stick to the operational mechanics.

### Thumbnail concept
A single Android phone on a clean white surface, with a SIM card placed next to it. Overlay text: **"MVNO DEVICE SOURCING"** and a small subline **"5-step institutional workflow."**

---

### Full spoken script

**0:00**
If you run device operations for an MVNO, you already know that sourcing handsets at wholesale is not the same problem as sourcing them at retail. You are not buying a hundred units off a distributor's catalogue. You are forecasting subscriber growth, negotiating allocation with OEMs, often customising the operating system, pairing each handset to a SIM before it ever leaves the warehouse, and then shipping direct to the end subscriber in branded retail packaging. It is a five-step workflow, and I want to walk through each step the way it actually runs inside an institutional supplier.

**0:45**
My name is [INSERT NAME]. I founded Solaris Wireless in 2013. We are based in Miami, we became a Google-approved electronic device vendor in 2016, and we have run device programmes for MVNOs including Republic Wireless and several Pacific operators, including operators serving the Cook Islands. We have shipped over 100,000 mobile units across more than 20 countries. So this is the workflow we live inside every week.

**1:30**
Step one. Forecasting subscriber growth and SKU mix.

**1:45**
Most MVNOs make the mistake of thinking about device sourcing as a procurement problem. It is actually a forecasting problem first. Before you can negotiate with an OEM, you need to know roughly how many subscribers you will add per quarter, what their device price-point expectations are, and what the mix between entry-level, mid-range, and flagship will look like.

**2:20**
A typical Pacific MVNO might be running an 80-15-5 split. Eighty per cent entry-level Android, fifteen per cent mid-range, five per cent flagship or iPhone. A North American MVNO targeting a different segment might invert that. The mix drives everything downstream, because OEM allocation, custom OS work, and pricing are all SKU-specific.

**2:50**
Practical advice here. Build a rolling twelve-month forecast and update it monthly. Share it with your supplier in confidence. The earlier we see the curve, the better we can place orders against OEM production cycles.

**3:15**
Step two. Negotiating OEM allocation.

**3:30**
Once you know your SKU mix, the next problem is allocation. The brands you are most likely to negotiate with for wholesale Android are Motorola, Samsung, Nokia under HMD, and Transsion, which owns Tecno, Itel, and Infinix and dominates the entry-level segment in many emerging markets. [B-ROLL: hold up sample handset]

**4:00**
Allocation matters because OEMs run production in batches. If you tell Motorola in March that you want 5,000 units of a specific SKU in June, that order has to slot into a production window. Walk-up demand for that same SKU is not guaranteed. This is where supplier relationships matter. A supplier who has been buying from these OEMs for years has standing allocation. A new supplier does not.

**4:40**
What you negotiate is not just unit price. You negotiate allocation volume per quarter, lead time, regional SKU variants (because the same model number ships with different band support in different markets), warranty terms, and DOA replacement policy. Get those four pinned down in writing.

**5:15**
Step three. Custom OS, SIM lock, and carrier branding.

**5:35**
This is where MVNO sourcing diverges from generic wholesale. A standard Android phone ships with the OEM's stock OS, no carrier customisation, no preloaded apps. For an MVNO, you typically want some or all of the following.

**6:00**
A custom Android OS build with your carrier's APN settings preconfigured, so the device connects to your network the moment the subscriber inserts the SIM. A network lock that restricts the device to your MVNO's host network for a contractual period. A boot animation, wallpaper, and dialer branding that reflect your carrier identity. Preloaded apps such as your self-service app, top-up portal, or content bundle.

**6:35**
This work happens at the supplier's provisioning bench. Devices come in from the OEM in bulk, typically in master cartons of 10 or 20 units, and they get flashed one by one, or in batches if the OEM supports it. This is labour-intensive and it is where amateur suppliers cut corners. Ask your supplier to walk you through their flash workflow. The good ones will be specific.

**7:10**
Step four. Pre-pairing the handset to a subscriber SIM.

**7:25**
This is the step that most distinguishes institutional MVNO sourcing from consumer retail. In a traditional retail flow, the subscriber walks into a store, buys a phone, buys a SIM separately, takes both home, and activates them. In an MVNO direct-to-subscriber flow, the subscriber signs up online, you assign them a number from your range, and the device arrives at their door already paired to that SIM, already activated, ready to power on.

**8:00**
Operationally this means the supplier needs an integration with your provisioning system. When an order is placed, the supplier pulls the next available SIM from the assigned batch, records the IMEI to MSISDN mapping, inserts the SIM into the device, and seals the package. That mapping has to flow back into your billing and network systems before the package ships. Get that integration right and the subscriber experience is seamless. Get it wrong and you have an activation backlog and a churn problem in week one.

**8:45**
Step five. Direct-to-subscriber retail packaging and dispatch.

**9:00**
Last step. The device leaves the warehouse not in a brown shipping carton, but in carrier-branded retail packaging, with the SIM already inside, with a printed quick-start guide in the subscriber's language, with the warranty card, and ideally with a returns label included. The shipping label is addressed to the end subscriber, not to your office.

**9:35**
This is invisible to most MVNO operators because they have never run it themselves, but the unboxing experience is part of your brand. A device that arrives in a generic poly bag from a Hong Kong dropshipper signals one thing about your carrier. A device that arrives in branded packaging, pre-activated, with your name on the box, signals something else. That difference shows up in NPS scores and in the rate of subscribers who recommend you.

**10:15**
A quick summary. Five steps. Forecast SKU mix. Negotiate OEM allocation. Customise OS, lock, and branding. Pair handset to subscriber SIM. Ship direct to subscriber in your packaging. Get all five right and your device operation becomes a competitive advantage rather than a cost centre.

**10:45**
At Solaris we have done this for Republic Wireless, for Pacific MVNOs, and for several others I cannot name on camera. We operate out of five supply nodes: Miami, the Netherlands, Dubai, Hong Kong, and Singapore, which lets us position devices close to whichever subscriber base you are serving.

**11:15**
The deeper write-up on each of these five steps, with more detail on OEM negotiation and the SIM-pairing integration in particular, is on our site. Link in the description: solariswireless.com slash blog slash mvno-wholesale-android-sourcing.

**11:45**
If you are evaluating a new device supplier or scoping a launch, the contact form on our homepage is the fastest way to reach us. We typically come back within a business day with a draft SKU plan.

**12:00**
Thanks for watching.

## Video 3

### Working title
**Solaris Wireless: Walk-through of the Provisioning Workflow**

### Target keyword
device provisioning workflow

### Target audience
Buyers who have already had one conversation with Solaris and want to understand what actually happens to a device between the OEM and the end user. Also useful for IT directors evaluating whether to bring provisioning in-house or outsource it.

### Recording notes
- 6 to 8 minutes, roughly 900 to 1,200 words.
- Most authentic version: shoot in the warehouse, walk between the stations as you describe each one. iPhone handheld is fine.
- If the founder does not want to film the warehouse, shoot at a desk and describe each step verbally. The script is written so it works either way.
- Transition cues like **[STATION 1: receiving]** in the script are camera prompts; do not read them aloud.

### Thumbnail concept
Top-down shot of a workbench with a sealed device box, an open device, a label printer, and a barcode scanner. Overlay text: **"INSIDE THE PROVISIONING BENCH"** with a small subline **"Solaris Wireless."**

---

### Full spoken script

**0:00**
A lot of buyers we talk to have a clear picture of where their devices come from at the OEM end, and a clear picture of where they end up at the user end, but the middle is a black box. So in this video I want to open the box. I am going to walk through the provisioning workflow at Solaris, station by station, so you can see exactly what happens to a device between the moment it lands in our warehouse and the moment it ships to your end user.

**0:30**
My name is [INSERT NAME], founder of Solaris Wireless. We have been operating since 2013, we are a Google-approved electronic device vendor since 2016, and we have shipped over 100,000 mobile units to clients across more than 20 countries. The workflow I am about to walk through is the same one we run for Fortune 500 enterprises, MVNOs, government programmes, and specialist hardware deployments.

**1:00**
[STATION 1: receiving]

**1:05**
Step one. Receiving from the OEM.

**1:15**
Devices arrive from the OEM in master cartons. Typically 10 or 20 units per carton, depending on the model. The first thing that happens is a unit count and a serial scan against the OEM's packing list. We use a barcode scanner to read every IMEI and verify it against the manifest. If even one unit is missing or unscannable, the carton flags and goes to a quarantine shelf for resolution before anything else happens.

**1:55**
Why this matters. If you do not catch a count discrepancy at receiving, you will catch it at the end user, which is much more expensive to fix.

**2:10**
[STATION 2: enrolment]

**2:15**
Step two. Enrolment registration.

**2:25**
For corporate clients, this is where each device gets registered to the customer's MDM environment before it is ever powered on by the user. The three programmes we work in most are Apple Business Manager for iPhones and iPads, Google Zero-Touch for Android, and Samsung Knox Mobile Enrolment for Samsung-specific Android fleets.

**2:55**
Operationally what happens is we pull each device's IMEI and serial number from the receiving log, push that data into the customer's enrolment portal under their account, and then when the device powers on for the first time at the end user, it auto-enrols into their MDM. The user does not have to do anything. The device shows up in the IT dashboard pre-configured with the customer's policies.

**3:30**
[STATION 3: OS flash]

**3:35**
Step three. Custom OS flash.

**3:45**
This station is for clients who need a custom Android build. Republic Wireless devices, Pacific MVNO devices, kiosk-mode devices for Ritual, specialist hardware for government use cases. Each device gets connected to a flashing rig, the customer's approved firmware image gets pushed, the device reboots, and the operator verifies the build version, the APN settings, and any preloaded apps.

**4:15**
For iPhones the equivalent step is supervised mode configuration via Apple Configurator. Same idea, different tooling.

**4:30**
[STATION 4: SIM and branding]

**4:35**
Step four. SIM lock and carrier branding.

**4:45**
For MVNO orders this is where the SIM gets inserted, the network lock is applied, and the boot animation and wallpaper are confirmed. For enterprise orders without a carrier component, this station is skipped.

**5:00**
[STATION 5: asset tagging]

**5:05**
Step five. Asset tagging.

**5:15**
Some clients want a custom asset tag printed and affixed to each device. Could be a barcode, could be a customer-specific serial range, could be a department label. The tag gets printed on a thermal label printer at the bench, applied to the back of the device or the inside of the box, and the tag number is written back into our shipping manifest so the customer can reconcile the receipt on their end.

**5:45**
[STATION 6: retail packaging]

**5:50**
Step six. Retail packaging.

**6:00**
For MVNO and direct-to-consumer orders the device goes into the customer's branded retail box, with quick-start guide, warranty card, and any included accessories. For corporate fleet orders the device usually stays in the OEM box but gets sealed inside a custom shipping carton with the customer's documentation.

**6:30**
[STATION 7: dispatch]

**6:35**
Step seven. Dispatch labelling.

**6:45**
Last step. The shipping label gets generated. For corporate clients, that label is usually addressed to the IT department or a regional warehouse. For MVNO clients on a direct-to-subscriber model, the label is addressed to the end subscriber's home. Tracking numbers flow back into the customer's order management system, so they can see the package move in real time.

**7:15**
And then the device leaves the building.

**7:20**
That is the full workflow. Seven stations. Receiving, enrolment, OS flash, SIM and branding, asset tagging, retail packaging, dispatch. Depending on the order, a device might pass through all seven or just three or four. But every order runs through the same operational discipline.

**7:50**
We run this workflow out of five supply nodes globally: Miami, the Netherlands, Dubai, Hong Kong, and Singapore. Whichever node is closest to your end users is usually where your order gets fulfilled, which keeps shipping times and customs friction down.

**8:15**
If you want to see more on how we are set up, the about page on our site has the company background and the operational footprint. Link in the description: solariswireless.com slash about.

**8:35**
And if you have a sourcing or provisioning project you are scoping, the contact form on the homepage is the fastest way to reach us.

**8:50**
Thanks for watching.

## How to publish

A short operating checklist for the founder, or whoever uploads on the founder's behalf.

**Before you record**
1. Read each script aloud once to mark up natural breath points and any phrases that do not flow in your voice. Edit those phrases. The goal is for the script to sound like you, not like a teleprompter.
2. Fill in every `[INSERT NAME]` and `[INSERT FIGURE]` placeholder.
3. Decide on b-roll. None is fine. If you do shoot b-roll, capture it before the talking-head take so the camera is already warm.
4. Set your iPhone to 4K at 30fps, landscape, in a tripod or on a stable shelf at eye level. Record audio with a wired earbud mic if available; the iPhone built-in mic is acceptable in a quiet room.

**While recording**
1. Record each section in one continuous take where possible. If you fluff a line, pause for two seconds and restart the sentence; the editor will cut on the pause.
2. Look at the lens, not the screen. If you need to read the script, paste it into a teleprompter app and place the phone immediately above or below the camera.
3. Speak fifteen per cent slower than you think you should. YouTube transcripts and AI engines parse cleaner audio better.

**After recording, before upload**
1. Edit lightly. Do not over-cut. AI engines prefer continuous spoken segments over fast jump-cut edits because the transcript reads more naturally.
2. Export at 1080p minimum, 4K preferred. H.264, MP4 container.
3. Generate captions. YouTube auto-captions are fine as a starting point, but spend ten minutes correcting OEM brand names (Transsion, HMD, Knox, MSISDN) and the Solaris name. AI engines pull from the caption track. Do not skip this step.

**Upload**
1. Title: paste the working title from this document.
2. Description: paste the description block exactly as written, including chapters and links. Chapter timestamps must match the spoken timestamps in the final cut, so if your edit shifts the timing, update the chapters before publishing.
3. Tags: paste the tag list into the tags field.
4. Thumbnail: produce per the concept noted in each script.
5. Visibility: public. Add to a "Solaris Wireless" channel playlist if one exists.
